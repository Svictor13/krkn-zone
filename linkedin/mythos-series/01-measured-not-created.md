# LinkedIn Post 1 — Publish: April 14, 2026

A 27-year-old bug in OpenBSD.
A 17-year-old root access hole in FreeBSD.
An FFmpeg flaw that survived five million automated tests.

Mythos didn't create these. They were always there.

Everyone's debating whether AI will "hack the world." NBC is calling it "Vulnpocalypse." The Pentagon is arguing about who controls the model.

None of that is the real story.

The real story is that the software billions of people use every day — software that passed decades of human review, automated scanning, and compliance audits — was broken the whole time.

We told ourselves our controls were working. Mythos held up a mirror and showed us the resistance strength we'd been modeling was built on assumptions that were never tested.

I've spent 25 years building risk programs across Latin America. The pattern is always the same: a green dashboard, a confident CISO, and a board that assumes green means safe.

Green means the tool didn't find anything. That's a very different statement.

Mythos found what our tools couldn't. Not because it's magic — because it looked harder than we ever did.

The model isn't the threat. The mirror is.

This is why FAIR — Factor Analysis of Information Risk — exists.

FAIR doesn't ask "how many vulnerabilities do you have?" It asks: what's the probability of loss? What's the financial magnitude? Are your controls actually working — or did they just pass over the same flaws for 27 years?

A CISO who walks into the boardroom after Mythos with the same vulnerability dashboard is presenting fiction. The board needs loss scenarios with probability distributions and financial ranges.

Mythos changed the inputs to every FAIR model on earth. The threat frequency just multiplied by orders of magnitude. The control effectiveness assumptions no longer hold. Every expected annual loss calculation your board approved last quarter needs to be rerun.

That's not a technology problem. It's a governance problem. And FAIR is the math that solves it.
