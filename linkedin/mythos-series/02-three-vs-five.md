# LinkedIn Post 2 — Publish: April 17, 2026

Every FAIR model you've ever run assumed the threat actor was a person.

A person with limited bandwidth. A person who sleeps, gets bored, decides the target isn't worth the effort. A person who targets one company at a time.

Mythos isn't a person.

It scored 83% on vulnerability reproduction. It generated working exploits 72% of the time. It found decades-old bugs in systems famous for their security culture. It wrote exploit chains that bypassed defenses nobody had ever seriously tested.

And it can do all of that simultaneously, across thousands of targets, around the clock.

Here's what that does to your FAIR model:

Contact frequency — the number of times a threat actor probes your environment — goes from "a few per quarter" to "continuous." Threat capability jumps from "skilled human" to "AI with a 72% exploit success rate." Resistance strength — which you calibrated against human attackers — turns out to have been dramatically overestimated, because Mythos found flaws that survived 27 years of human review and five million automated tests.

Increase contact frequency by orders of magnitude. Increase threat capability. Decrease resistance strength. Run the Monte Carlo.

The expected annual loss that your board approved last year is no longer defensible.

I've deployed FAIR across eight countries. The pattern is always the same: before you run the numbers, the CISO feels confident. After you run the numbers, the conversation changes.

Mythos just changed the inputs. The question is whether you've rerun the math.

Stop debating whether AI is dangerous. Run the FAIR model and let the loss exceedance curve speak for itself.
